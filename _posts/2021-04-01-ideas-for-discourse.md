I've been thinking about online discourse a lot lately.

Twitter, as a platform, is a platform for _shouting_. Not _talking_, _shouting_. I don't know exactly why Twitter has ended up this way, but I can at least throw pennies from the peanut gallery. It's not only Twitter that's ended up as a shouting match, but it's the example that I think of most.

## Dunking
One element on Twitter that definitely has not improved discourse is the *dunk*. What is *dunking*?

Imagine the most terrible take you can on a subject that someone has written years ago. Now imagine that person is engaged in a debate with someone on an entirely different subject, and the other person has no rebuttal. When the other person makes a *quote tweet* with the terrible take appended to it, simply saying "this u?", it does not create a good argument. It's a false argument. This is *dunking*.

A good platform would not allow dunking. Not by *rule*, but by *making it impossible*. In the absence of a "dunking detector" that prevents dunking, there should be a quota on using the quote retweet button. *If you want to dunk on people, you best make it count.*

## Permanence
Another thought I've had is the idea of self-destructing posts. I don't believe that someone's stupid takes should be held against them forever. They should be free to hide their posts just in case someone decides to root through all of their past tweets that they made when they were less developed. This does come at a cost, though: it makes keeping a historical record about a user difficult. *What if an anonymous account with an anime cat avatar successfully predicted the next pandemic? Wouldn't we want to keep a record of this?*

I'm proposing the ability to *hide* tweets, not *delete* them. I'm willing to go further: if Twitter was able to keep a summary of a tweet, and another user asks Twitter to show a tweet from a user that contained a specific word (or matched the summary), why shouldn't Twitter show that tweet to that user (privately) with the approval of the poster? The poster wouldn't know who requested it, only that someone did request hidden tweets matching a specific description. I'm convinced this is possible; we have [good language models](https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2020/11/summarize-twitter-live-data-using-pretrained-nlp-models/) that allow Twitter to "compress" a tweet into a description.

## Doxxing
One common event that happens on the underbelly of Twitter is an intrepid reporter (or a self-imagined intrepid reporter) writing an expose on a popular anonymous Twitter user. This is known as *doxxing*, which is another problem in itself.

Doxxing has no place on any platform. Why Twitter tolerates it is beyond me. Please don't give me the "it's OK when we do it" nonsense. If you dox someone, other people will think it's OK to dox you. A strict no-doxxing rule should be in place. *Nobody is allowed to reveal personal information like name, address, employment about someone who hasn't already discolosed these things on that platform*.

Please don't conflate this with reporting wrongdoing. It's certainly right for users to point out that another user is acting immorally or unethically. *I'm only suggesting that users leave the "visiting someone's home" part to the authorities.*

## Upvotes
On Twitter, there's no downside to having a terrible opinion, or articulating an opinion terribly. There's no way for other users to say "no, that is a bad take" with a button in the same way as they can voice approval with the *like* and *retweet* button.

Sure, there's the *ratio*; if a tweet you make gets more replies than likes, that's a *ratio*. It's not the same. One thing I can give Facebook credit for is the addition of the "angry" emote button. Twitter should have one. For the love of all that is holy, don't let your data scientists A/B test it; they've damaged society's dopamine receptors enough already.

## Clout
If you have the magical Twitter Blue Tick, that doesn't solely mean that Twitter can vouch that you are who you say you are. There are rules you have to follow if you want your little blue tick. You can't just send a copy of your passport and get a blue tick that verifies you are John Smith. You have to be someone who Twitter *likes*.

A side-effect of this is that interactions between blue-ticks and non-ticks can be unpleasant, and nine times out of ten, the blue-tick gets a warning where a non-tick would cop a ban.

Twitter should give everyone a fair banning where rules are broken. Fix the verification system! A blue tick should be verification, and verification should be available to everyone who has an ID.

## Anonymity
Anonymity has its place. If it wasn't for anonymity and anonymous speech, the world would be a far darker place. Freedom is seldom pretty, however. Some truly horrific things get said while wearing a mask. Look at the detritus what was on 8chan. Not that it stops people on Facebook from saying horrible things, and Facebook has a real-names policy!

Here's a quick solution: allow named people to block content from anonymous users, but also allow anon users to block content from verified users. If you're into a hermetically-sealed bubble where commoners don't talk to "elites", that's your thing: use this feature. You don't have to listen to abuse from anime avatars, and they don't have to hear whining from blue ticks.

## Usernames
Another radical proposal: *don't let people pick their usernames*. I don't mean a random string of numbers, but rather a two-word simple name from random words, like `@babylon-porter`. One approach is do what [urbit does for usernames and generate them from the user-id](https://urbit.org/understanding-urbit/urbit-id/). I'd also allow users to regenerate their username a maximum of N times.

Why? One issue that does pop up with letting people choose their usernames is that people's accounts get hacked *solely for their username*. This also helps with the Clout problem: someone who is big on another platform should stick to that platform; they shouldn't transfer their clout over to other platforms. If you're big on YouTube, and you start an account on Twitter, Twitter should be at least aware that you're bringing a crowd over from YouTube. You may even get a blue tick!

Let the blue ticks keep their usernames, provided they can verify who they are. The price of anonymity is that you don't get to import your brand from another platform. Is that a fair deal?
