Here's a radical thought for you: in the future, you might not work for a corporation. You might not post to a central server. The car in which you travel might own itself, and act as a sovereign agent, able to arrange its own repairs and ferry people around. Yet, you won't see a difference to your life. In fact, it might even be much better than it is now.

## Culture Follows Technology, People Follow Culture
Let's start this train of thought from the beginning. How do you tell where the future's going? Is there a way to stick your finger in the air and tell what way the technological wind is blowing?

One heuristic is to look at what technology is doing *now*, and extrapolate it *five years* into the future. What's possible now that wasn't possible five years ago?

Five years ago, you could upload high-definition videos to YouTube. Google Maps on Android was getting smart enough to tell you where the nearest petrol station was. It was the final days of the Old YouTube, where originality and innovation was rewarded by the YouTube algorithms. You could order a car to pick you up from Liverpool Street Station to drop you off at your AirBNB in Westminster. You could send money through Bitcoin, PayPal, or whatever app your bank has picked up a stake in. You could express your displeasure at all this on Twitter, before taking a picture of your overpriced Latte on Instagram.

How about now? Has there been any progress?

You can still upload high-definition videos to YouTube. In fact, you can upload 4K video now. Google Maps now knows where Wetherspoons is. YouTube still remains where content is hosted, although public opinion on YouTube is polarised these days. Uber's appeaing a ban with Transport For London. AirBNB is still kicking around, although hosts and guests are advised to not tell people that they're using AirBNB. There are more cryptocurrency platforms around now-- Bitcoin, Ethereum, Monero. PayPal's becoming irrelevant. But post this on Twitter, and you realise how polarised the world is now. Instagram's trying to sell you overpriced latte.

If you think about it, you already had self driving cars. It's just that there was another guy sat in the car with you. The perk of a self driving car is that the taxi company doesn't have to pay for the driver, not so much that you get to sit in a self-driving car.

Where's the change here? I don't think that technology's changed all that much, at least for users. There's been a tonne of innovation with back-end and technical matters, but the user rarely sees this. So why have people changed their minds all of a sudden?

## Society Catching Up
I believe that society is only just catching up to technology. It took society a while, but the opinion of people I talk to is that some aspects of technology have not been beneficial for the people at large.

Here's how I view it: there's a lag between cause and effect, more so when it affects large chunks of the population. It took ten years for cryptocurrency to become mainstream, and even then, the notion of digitally-secured digital tokens outside the issuance of the Royal Mint was first proposed by David Chaum. People didn't learn about Artificial Intelligence until it started to affect them personally, when they were shown a peek behind the data curtain. Robotics used to be the preserve of agricultural engineers, universities, NASA, and the army until Google started to work on autonomous passenger vehicles. Now that the public at large is exposed to this technology, the culture has shifted.

With that in mind: what's being developed now that your barber will ask about in five years?

## Technology Isn't Bad, Centralisation Is Bad
If you ask the common lady or gentleman on the street what their view on technology is, many of them will have mixed feelings on it. They'll tell you that they can use WhatsApp to talk to their families, which they like, and they're able to ask their local Facebook Group (let's call this *What's Happening In Little And Greater Buggering*) whether they've seen their pet cat. They can order a taxi through an app, and they can order a phone charger through Amazon and have it delivered the next day, regardless of whether they live in London or in West Norfolk.

However, they'll also tell you that they've known people who have been bullied on the internet. Perhaps it's their sons and daughters, who were bullied on social media platforms for liking the wrong things. Perhaps it's their cousin, who posted something that perhaps wasn't to people's social mores, and received a social media dog-pile for having the wrong opinion. They'll also tell you that they knew someone who was the victim of identity fraud, or that they knew someone who was groomed by a nonce through Instagram. Their uncle lost his job as a taxi driver because the taxi app company found a way to employ a driver without paying their national insurance, and their wife's sister's shop closed down because Amazon is cheaper.

It's clear, then, what has happened. A slick user experience, combined with a centralised corporation in control of platforms, has resulted in corporations sucking the life out of entire postcodes. The users love the experience, but hate the side-effects. The technology isn't to blame here. The concept of centralisation is. How can we make this better?

## Take The UX, Leave The Centralisation
The user experience of modernity doesn't need much improvement. It was designed over a period of hundreds of man-years, through the mechanism of the A/B test. Don't ask a user what they would like. Find out what they like. In the example above, the common lady or gentleman would advocate the removal of this technology. This is wrong. They like the technology. They don't like what it does when it doesn't work for the community.

I would keep the user interface. Let people buy things through apps. Let people post things on to community groups. Let people order a cab through an app. This, in itself, is not the harmful bit. The harm comes in when the marketplace is able to distort the market so that it can squeeze more profit for the owner. We have a word for that: *Rent-seeking*. How can we get rid of rent-seeking?

## Legislation Won't Work
It has long been suggested that owners of products and marketplaces should be made to split the legal entity of the corporation into halves: one for the product, one for the marketplace. The idea is that the corporation should not be able to wash one hand with the other, by allowing data from the marketplace to be used to favour the distribution of their products at the expense of their competitors. By partitioning the corporation, the market has no incentive to rig the scales so their products end up at the top of the list.

This won't work. I fear it's too late. The owners of products and marketplace companies are able to outspend the small shops of Little Buggering. The second that a large state power threatens legislative action will be the second that the lobbyists are activated. Any legislation that has the slightest chance of working will be neutered, and it will ultimately end up harming the small shops of Little Buggering. This naïve approach will not work for this reason: the rent-seekers don't just occupy boardrooms; they occupy Parliament as well.

## Fundamentally Incapable Of Rent-Seeking
The clearest solution to me seems to be a Schumpeterian one: *Creative Destruction*.

Instead of trying to play in the rent-seeker's little sandbox in the hope that the rent-seeker will just tax you and not try to drive your product out of existence, why not just move to a much better sandbox?

I've seen plenty of good work in this area. The Ur-example is Bitcoin, which allowed the internet to have a payments layer which wasn't operated by a corporation. The principal innovation of Bitcoin was the concept of a *Blockchain*, which allowed a permanent record kept by as many participants in the network as possible, meaning that anyone wanting to change the record would have to coerce all of those participants.

This, to me, is *incapable of rent-seeking*. No one person can control Bitcoin. If the developers made unwise moves, users could use a fork (and lord knows how many Bitcoin forks there are these days). It's an innovative approach to how you think of money, and how it doesn't have to depend on there only being physically one of a banknote, only that the banknote is pinned to something that cost effort to make.

What other things like *Bitcoin* are there for the use-cases described above? I've seen attempts at general purpose computation based on this (Ethereum), with things built on top of a Turing-complete blockchain-based programming language. I'm particularly interested in Twetch, which uses the Bitcoin SV blockchain to store posts at a cost of X per post (and X per interaction with that post), creating an environment where if you post something, it best be worth something.

How about the purchase and sale of physical goods? There was an attempt at building a decentralised marketplace for many things called OpenBazaar, now built into the Haven app.

The only snag: people haven't heard of these new toys. *Yet.* They've heard of Bitcoin, they may well have heard of Ethereum, but it will be a good few years before they hear of Twetch and OpenBazaar.

## How To Encourage Adoption
If you want someone to use something, you should make it more than simple: you should make it *an absolute joy* to use.

Here's an example: why do people hate online banking? Honestly, it feels like a complete chore. You have to use a digital two-step token to gain access. When you do, it's *slow*. Everything takes more than *three clicks*. Along came some of the new "challenger" banks, which introduced apps that you can put on your phone to manage your money.

Here's another example: ordering a cab through your phone. In the olden days, you had to ring up to see if there were any cabs around. If there were, you had to wait, before getting in the cab, having chapter and verse on the topic of *immigration* delivered to the driver while he took the long way around to run up the meter, and if you wanted to pay with a card, you would be told that the card machine was broken. After Uber, though? You can see if there are any cabs nearby. You ordered it through the phone. It would pick you up, nice and clean, with a friendly driver, and if you were pleasant he would give you a good rating, and if he was pleasant you could give him a good rating.

## Decentralisation Now
I do believe decentralisation is inevitable. Corporations and their enablers will resist the tide, but there's no stopping it now.

If you don't believe me: look at `git`, the world's most popular version control system. It's decentralised. It became decentralised because the Linux kernel team wanted a decentralised workflow. They found centralised systems like `svn` to be unperformant. Some operations on `git`, like interactive rebasing locally, are not possible without keeping a copy of the history locally.

Nobody uses `svn` anymore because `git` is nicer. `git` works better for smaller and larger teams. Billion-dollar companies formed around it. This remains possible with any decentralised software project. Look at Bitcoin.
